Ford could ship its vehicles without chips, requiring dealers to complete the build

Ford is facing a production-halting chip shortage and is allegedly proposing what seems like a rather drastic measure to keep models flowing through to customers. The Blue Oval has apparently floated the idea of moving forward with delivery of new vehicles to dealerships without chips installed. Instead, the dealership technicians would be responsible for installing the chips on-site once they arrive. Not only does this put a very important aspect of the build process squarely on the oft-overcrowded plate of the local dealer technician, but it also puts a financial strain on dealers (and off of Ford) by essentially forcing them to hold vehicles on lots for longer than usual.

There is absolutely no indication of what led up to this outburst of violent behavior from an Ontario motorcyclist earlier this month. All we see is what the OPP has published to its Twitter account: a motorcyclist getting angry at the people in a car travelling in the lane beside it, eventually spitting on the car’s hood and then punching its side mirror loose before speeding off. But does it matter what happened beforehand? What type of driving behaviour justifies having a mirror punched loose? Ontario police are looking for anyone who may have information about the event or the rider involved.

Spy photos reveal mid-range Toyota Tundra details

The men who hide in bushes with cameras have come through for us again, this time snapping some revealing pics of the upcoming Toyota Tundra in its previously withheld mid-range setup. A sizable infotainment screen dominates the dash above some actual pressable buttons, while the tachometer shows a redline at 5,750 rpm, indicating the current engine will remain as an option in addition to a hybrid powertrain. Toyota is doing its best to hide whatever is going on around the half-ton pickup’s rear end with the tarp treatment, which itself hints at some sort of reworked suspension. All-new BMW i4 to start just under $55k in Canada

BMW Canada has uploaded the new i4 options to its website, and with that, it has opened orders, launched a vehicle configurator, and revealed prices for the 2022 i4 eDrive40 and i4 M50. Entry into the 2022 model-year i4 in Canada comes at $54,990 for the rear-wheel-drive, 335-horsepower eDrive40, and leaps up to $72,990 for the 536-horsepower, all-wheel-drive i4 M50. The 2022 BMW i4 is scheduled to arrive in Q1 of 2022.
